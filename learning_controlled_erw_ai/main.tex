\begin{abstract}
The elephant random walk is a non-Markovian stochastic process whose next
increment is drawn from its complete history and is then repeated or reversed.
This article introduces a learning-controlled elephant random walk (LC-ERW), in
which the memory parameter is no longer fixed but is selected online by a
predictable learning rule. The construction keeps complete elephant memory while
allowing the repeat--reverse probability to be generated by an adaptive policy.
The first main result is an exact martingale normalisation valid for arbitrary
predictable memory policies. The second main result is a functional invariance
principle: under a stable diffusive learning condition, the whole process
\(S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}/\sqrt n\) converges to the explicit Gaussian process
\(t^aB(t^{1-2a}/(1-2a))\). The third main result treats the learned critical
boundary \(a_n\to1/2\), proving both the central limit theorem under the
\(\sqrt{n\log n}\) normalisation and a critical functional limit with rank-one
Gaussian limit \(\sqrt t\,N\). The fourth main result is a quenched
universality principle: throughout a stable class of realised learning paths,
the diffusive limit depends only on the learned terminal coefficient \(a\), not
on the algorithmic trajectory by which \(a_n\) approaches \(a\). A projected
online empirical-risk learner is then shown to satisfy the assumptions, giving a
concrete adaptive policy rather than an exogenous deterministic schedule.
Reproducible Python and R scripts generate Monte Carlo diagnostics and plots
included in the manuscript.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} elephant random walk; artificial intelligence;
online learning; martingales; central limit theorem; non-Markovian random walk.

\section{Introduction}

Random walks with memory provide simple models in which local moves are affected
by a long historical record. The elephant random walk (ERW), introduced by
\citet{schutz2004elephants}, is a canonical example: at each time the walker
selects one of its past increments and either repeats or reverses it. This
mechanism generates diffusive, critical and superdiffusive regimes, depending on
the memory parameter. Subsequent mathematical work has developed central limit
theorems, martingale methods, urn connections and variants with restricted
memory, random step sizes and delays \citep{colette2017central,bercu2018martingale,baur2016elephant,gut2021variations,gut2023review,dedecker2023rates,aguech2024clt,aguech2024delays}.

Artificial intelligence (AI), particularly online learning and reinforcement
learning, is also concerned with sequential decisions under uncertainty. In many
algorithms, exploration is controlled by a parameter that is updated from the
past. Yet the usual random-walk component of such methods is often Markovian or
externally scheduled. This paper studies a complementary question: what happens
when the memory parameter of an ERW is itself produced by a learning rule?

The contribution is a learning-controlled elephant random walk (LC-ERW). The
walker has complete elephant memory, but the probability of repeating a recalled
increment is chosen from the past by an arbitrary predictable learning
algorithm. This includes deterministic schedules, online estimators, stochastic
approximation recursions and bounded policy maps. The model is deliberately
minimal: it is rich enough to represent AI-style adaptation, but constrained
enough to support rigorous proofs.

The results are as follows. First, for any predictable memory policy, the
LC-ERW admits an exact martingale normalisation. Secondly, if the learned memory
coefficient converges fast enough to a diffusive value \(a<1/2\), then the
normalised position \(S_n/\sqrt{n}\) converges to a centred normal distribution
with variance \((1-2a)^{-1}\). Thirdly, in the non-negative diffusive subregime
\(0\leq a<1/2\), the entire trajectory satisfies a functional invariance
principle with covariance
\[
  \E[G(s)G(t)]=\frac{s^{1-a}t^a}{1-2a},\qquad 0<s\leq t\leq1.
\]
Fourthly, at the learned critical boundary \(a_n\to1/2\), the paper proves
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}\Rightarrow\mathcal N(0,1).
\]
It also identifies the critical finite-dimensional path limit
\(S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}/\sqrt{n\log n}\Rightarrow\sqrt t\,N\). Fifthly, the
convergence is quenched over the stable learning class: different
online algorithms with the same limiting memory coefficient share the same
first-order diffusive law. Finally, a projected online empirical-risk learner is
proved to satisfy the stability assumptions. The numerical section supplies
reproducible Python and R code that checks the predicted variance and visualises
the Gaussian scaling.

The manuscript is not a claim of peer-reviewed novelty. Rather, it states a
precise candidate contribution, proves it under transparent assumptions and
provides scripts that make the quantitative claims testable.

\section{Related Literature}

\citet{schutz2004elephants} introduced the ERW as a solvable non-Markovian
model with long-range memory. Limit theorems and related asymptotic properties
were developed by \citet{colette2017central}, while \citet{bercu2018martingale}
showed that martingale methods give a particularly effective route to strong
laws, quadratic strong laws, laws of the iterated logarithm and non-Gaussian
behaviour in the superdiffusive regime.

The connection with urn models was clarified by \citet{baur2016elephant}, and
extensions have since become an active topic. \citet{gut2021variations} and
\citet{gut2023review} discuss variants with modified memory mechanisms.
\citet{dedecker2023rates} study random step sizes and rates of convergence,
while \citet{aguech2024clt} and \citet{aguech2024delays} examine gradually
increasing memory, random step sizes and delays. Reinforced and amnesic
variants modify the way past increments are sampled or weighted
\citep{laulin2022reinforced,randommemory2025}. In parallel, generalized and
randomized urn schemes have a mature theory based on branching-process and
stochastic-approximation methods
\citep{janson2004urns,janson2005triangular,pemantle2007survey,laruelle2013urns}.
These papers motivate the present study but do not, to the best of our
knowledge, formulate the ERW repeat--reverse probability itself as a predictable
output of an online learning policy while retaining complete ERW recall.

Table~\ref{tab:novelty} summarises the distinction. The proposed LC-ERW does
not claim to dominate these models; rather, it isolates a different source of
adaptivity. The increments remain the classical elephant increments, the recalled
index is still uniform over the full past, and the new adaptive object is only
the repeat--reverse coefficient. This separation is useful: it prevents the
model from being absorbed into random-memory ERW, where the remembered set is
randomised, or into adaptive urn schemes, where the composition/replacement
mechanism is the state variable. Here the learning state is external to the
elephant path but predictable from it, and it changes the conditional drift
coefficient \(a_nS_n/n\) directly.

\section{The Learning-Controlled Model}

Let \((\Omega,\F,\Pbb)\) be a probability space with filtration
\((\F_n)_{n\geq 1}\). The position of the walker after \(n\) steps is
\[
  S_n=\sum_{j=1}^n X_j,
  \qquad X_j\in\{-1,1\}.
\]
The first increment satisfies
\(\Pbb(X_1=1)=q\) and \(\Pbb(X_1=-1)=1-q\), where \(q\in[0,1]\).

For \(n\geq 1\), let \(K_n\) be conditionally uniform on
\(\{1,\ldots,n\}\) given \(\F_n\), and let \(p_n\) be an
\(\F_n\)-measurable random variable taking values in \((0,1)\). Conditional on
\(\F_n\) and \(K_n\), define
\[
  X_{n+1}=
  \begin{cases}
  X_{K_n}, & \text{with probability } p_n,\\
  -X_{K_n}, & \text{with probability } 1-p_n.
  \end{cases}
\]
The process \(p_n\) is the learning-controlled memory policy. In AI language,
it may be written as
\[
  p_n=\ell(\theta_n),
\]
where \(\theta_n\) is the state of an online learning algorithm and
\(\ell:\R^d\to(0,1)\) is a bounded policy link, for example a logistic map.
Set
\[
  a_n=2p_n-1.
\]
The coefficient \(a_n\in(-1,1)\) is the learned memory coefficient. Positive
values favour repetition of the recalled step, negative values favour reversal,
and \(a_n=0\) corresponds to no directional memory in conditional mean.

\begin{assumption}[Stable diffusive learning]\label{ass:stable}
There exist constants \(a\in(-1,1/2)\), \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\),
\(\bar a<1/2\) and \(D<\infty\) such that, almost surely,
\[
  a_n\in[-1+\varepsilon,\,1-\varepsilon]
  \quad\text{and}\quad a_n\leq\bar a
  \quad\text{for all } n,
\]
and
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{|a_n-a|}{n}\leq D.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
The summability condition is satisfied whenever \(a_n\to a\) at any polynomial
rate, for instance \(|a_n-a|=O(n^{-\rho})\) with \(\rho>0\). It is deliberately
stated pathwise, because the learning rule may be deterministic, data-driven or
generated by a stochastic approximation algorithm.
\end{remark}

\begin{assumption}[Stable critical learning]\label{ass:critical}
There exist constants \(\varepsilon\in(0,1)\) and \(D_c<\infty\) such that,
almost surely,
\[
  a_n\in[-1+\varepsilon,\,1-\varepsilon],
  \qquad a_n\leq\frac12
  \quad\text{for all }n,
\]
and
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{|a_n-1/2|}{n}\leq D_c.
\]
\end{assumption}

\section{Main Results}

\begin{theorem}[Exact martingale normalisation]\label{thm:martingale}
Define \(A_1=1\) and, for \(n\geq 1\),
\[
  A_{n+1}=\prod_{k=1}^{n}\left(1+\frac{a_k}{k}\right).
\]
Then
\[
  M_n=\frac{S_n}{A_n},\qquad n\geq 1,
\]
is a martingale with respect to \((\F_n)\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since \(K_n\) is conditionally uniform on \(\{1,\ldots,n\}\),
\[
  \E[X_{K_n}\mid \F_n]=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}X_j=\frac{S_n}{n}.
\]
Using the conditional repeat--reverse rule,
\[
  \E[X_{n+1}\mid \F_n]
  =(p_n-(1-p_n))\frac{S_n}{n}
  =a_n\frac{S_n}{n}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \E[S_{n+1}\mid \F_n]
  =S_n+a_n\frac{S_n}{n}
  =\left(1+\frac{a_n}{n}\right)S_n.
\]
By definition, \(A_{n+1}=(1+a_n/n)A_n\). Hence
\[
  \E\left[\frac{S_{n+1}}{A_{n+1}}\middle|\F_n\right]
  =\frac{S_n}{A_n}=M_n.
\]
The increments are bounded after normalisation on every finite horizon, so
integrability is immediate.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Asymptotics of the normalising product]\label{lem:product}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}, there is an almost surely finite random
variable \(L\in(0,\infty)\) such that
\[
  A_n=L n^a(1+o(1))\qquad\text{almost surely.}
\]
Moreover, there exist deterministic constants \(0<c_A<C_A<\infty\) such that
\[
  c_A n^a\leq A_n\leq C_A n^a
  \qquad\text{for all } n\geq1
\]
on the event where Assumption~\ref{ass:stable} holds.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
  \log A_n-a\log n
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\left\{
  \log\left(1+\frac{a_k}{k}\right)-\frac{a}{k}
  \right\}
  +a\left(\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac1k-\log n\right).
\]
The last term converges because the harmonic numbers satisfy
\(\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k^{-1}-\log n\to\gamma\), where \(\gamma\) is Euler's
constant. For the first term, decompose
\[
  \log\left(1+\frac{a_k}{k}\right)-\frac{a}{k}
  =
  \frac{a_k-a}{k}
  +r_k,
  \qquad
  r_k=\log\left(1+\frac{a_k}{k}\right)-\frac{a_k}{k}.
\]
Because \(a_k\in[-1+\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]\), the Taylor remainder is
uniformly bounded: there is \(C_\varepsilon<\infty\) such that
\(|r_k|\leq C_\varepsilon k^{-2}\) for all \(k\). Hence
\(\sum_k r_k\) converges absolutely, while
\(\sum_k (a_k-a)/k\) converges absolutely by Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}. Thus
\(\log A_n-a\log n\) converges to a finite real limit, say \(\log L\), which
proves \(A_n/(n^a)\to L\in(0,\infty)\).

The same estimates give deterministic bounds. Indeed,
\[
  \left|\log A_n-a\log n\right|
  \leq
  D+C_\varepsilon\sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-2}
  +|a|\sup_{m\geq1}\left|\sum_{k=1}^{m}\frac1k-\log(m+1)\right|.
\]
Exponentiating this bound gives the constants \(c_A\) and \(C_A\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Second moment control]\label{lem:second}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}, there is a deterministic constant \(C_2\)
such that
\[
  \sup_{n\geq 1}\frac{\E[S_n^2]}{n}\leq C_2.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(V_n=\E[S_n^2]\). Since \(X_{n+1}^2=1\),
\[
  \E[S_{n+1}^2\mid\F_n]
  =S_n^2+2S_n\E[X_{n+1}\mid\F_n]+1
  =\left(1+\frac{2a_n}{n}\right)S_n^2+1.
\]
Taking expectations and using \(a_n\leq\bar a<1/2\) gives
\[
  V_{n+1}\leq \left(1+\frac{2\bar a}{n}\right)V_n+1.
\]
Set \(\beta=2\bar a<1\). Iterating the recursion yields
\[
  V_n
  \leq
  V_1\prod_{j=1}^{n-1}\left(1+\frac{\beta}{j}\right)
  +\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \prod_{j=k+1}^{n-1}\left(1+\frac{\beta}{j}\right).
\]
There is \(C_\beta<\infty\) such that
\(\prod_{j=k+1}^{n-1}(1+\beta/j)\leq C_\beta(n/k)^\beta\). Therefore
\[
  V_n\leq C_\beta n^\beta
  +C_\beta n^\beta\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}k^{-\beta}
  \leq C_2 n,
\]
because \(\beta<1\). This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Predictable quadratic variation]\label{lem:bracket}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:stable},
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\langle M\rangle_n
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}
  \frac{1}{1-2a}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The martingale increments are
\[
  \Delta M_{k+1}
  =
  \frac{X_{k+1}-a_kS_k/k}{A_{k+1}}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
  \langle M\rangle_n
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \frac{\E[(X_{k+1}-a_kS_k/k)^2\mid\F_k]}{A_{k+1}^2}
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \frac{1-a_k^2S_k^2/k^2}{A_{k+1}^2}.
\]
We first evaluate the leading term. Put \(B_n=A_n/n^a\). By
Lemma~\ref{lem:product}, \(B_n\to L\in(0,\infty)\) almost surely. Hence
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac{1}{A_{k+1}^2}
  =
  B_n^2 n^{2a-1}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac{1}{B_{k+1}^2(k+1)^{2a}}.
\]
Since \(B_{k+1}^{-2}\to L^{-2}\) and \(2a<1\), Cesaro summation for regularly
varying sequences gives
\[
  n^{2a-1}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac{1}{B_{k+1}^2(k+1)^{2a}}
  \longrightarrow
  \frac{L^{-2}}{1-2a}.
\]
Multiplication by \(B_n^2\to L^2\) gives
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac{1}{A_{k+1}^2}
  \longrightarrow
  \frac{1}{1-2a}
  \qquad\text{almost surely.}
\]

It remains to show that the correction term is negligible. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:product}, \(|a_k|\leq1\) and Lemma~\ref{lem:second},
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \E\left[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \frac{a_k^2S_k^2/k^2}{A_{k+1}^2}
  \right]
  &\leq
  C n^{2a-1}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} k^{-2a-2}\E[S_k^2]  \\
  &\leq
  C n^{2a-1}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} k^{-2a-1}.
\end{aligned}
\]
The final expression tends to zero in all cases \(a<1/2\): if \(a>0\), the
sum is bounded; if \(a=0\), it is \(O(\log n)\); and if \(a<0\), it is
\(O(n^{-2a})\), giving \(O(n^{-1})\). Thus the correction term converges to
zero in \(L^1\), and hence in probability. The lemma follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Lindeberg condition]\label{lem:lindeberg}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}, for every \(\delta>0\),
\[
  \frac{1}{\langle M\rangle_n}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \E\left[(\Delta M_{k+1})^2
  \1_{\{|\Delta M_{k+1}|>\delta\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}\}}
  \middle|\F_k\right]
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}0.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Because \(|X_{k+1}|\leq1\), \(|a_k|\leq1\), and \(|S_k|\leq k\),
\[
  |\Delta M_{k+1}|
  =
  \frac{|X_{k+1}-a_kS_k/k|}{A_{k+1}}
  \leq \frac{2}{A_{k+1}}.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:product} gives \(A_k\asymp k^a\). If \(a\geq0\), then
\(\max_{k\leq n}|\Delta M_k|=O(1)\); if \(a<0\), then
\(\max_{k\leq n}|\Delta M_k|=O(n^{-a})\). On the other hand,
Lemma~\ref{lem:bracket} implies
\[
  \langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}
  \asymp n^{1/2-a}
  \qquad\text{in probability.}
\]
Therefore
\[
  \frac{\max_{k\leq n}|\Delta M_k|}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}0.
\]
With probability tending to one, every indicator in the Lindeberg sum is then
zero, which proves the assertion.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Diffusive central limit theorem]\label{thm:clt}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:stable},
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt{n}}
  \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathcal{D}\ }\ 
  \mathcal{N}\left(0,\frac{1}{1-2a}\right).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The martingale \(M_n=S_n/A_n\) has increments satisfying the conditional
Lindeberg condition by Lemma~\ref{lem:lindeberg}. Since
\(\langle M\rangle_n\to\infty\) in probability by Lemmas~\ref{lem:product} and
\ref{lem:bracket}, the martingale central limit theorem
\citep[Theorem 3.2, for instance]{hall1980martingale} gives
\[
  \frac{M_n-M_1}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}\mathcal N(0,1).
\]
Moreover \(M_1/\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}\to0\) in probability. Hence
\[
  \frac{M_n}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}\mathcal N(0,1).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:bracket} states that
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\langle M\rangle_n
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}
  \frac{1}{1-2a}.
\]
Since \(S_n=A_nM_n\), Slutsky's theorem yields
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt n}
  =
  \frac{M_n}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \left(\frac{A_n^2\langle M\rangle_n}{n}\right)^{1/2}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}
  \mathcal N\left(0,\frac{1}{1-2a}\right).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Functional invariance principle]\label{thm:fclt}
Assume Assumption~\ref{ass:stable} and suppose in addition that
\(0\leq a<1/2\). Define the cĂ dlĂ g process
\[
  Y_n(t)=\frac{S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{\sqrt n},
  \qquad 0\leq t\leq1,
\]
with \(S_0=0\). Then \(Y_n\) converges in distribution in \(D[0,1]\) to the
centred Gaussian process
\[
  G(t)=t^a B\left(\frac{t^{1-2a}}{1-2a}\right),
  \qquad 0\leq t\leq1,
\]
where \(B\) is a standard Brownian motion. Equivalently, for
\(0<s\leq t\leq1\),
\[
  \E[G(s)G(t)]
  =
  \frac{s^{1-a}t^a}{1-2a}.
\]
The linearly interpolated version of \(Y_n\) converges to the same limit in
\(C[0,1]\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We give the details because this result is the main strengthening over the
one-dimensional CLT. Set
\[
  \mathcal M_n(t)
  =
  \frac{A_n}{\sqrt n}\left(M_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}-M_1\right),
  \qquad 0\leq t\leq1.
\]
The predictable quadratic variation of \(\mathcal M_n\) is
\[
  \left\langle\mathcal M_n\right\rangle(t)
  =
  \frac{A_n^2}{n}\langle M\rangle_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}.
\]
The proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:bracket}, with the upper summation limit replaced
by \(\lfloor nt\rfloor\), gives for each \(t\in[0,1]\)
\[
  \left\langle\mathcal M_n\right\rangle(t)
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}
  \frac{t^{1-2a}}{1-2a}.
\]
The convergence is uniform on compact subintervals of \([0,1]\), because the
leading term is a regularly varying partial sum and the correction term is
bounded in \(L^1\) by the corresponding full sum in Lemma~\ref{lem:bracket}.

The jumps of \(\mathcal M_n\) are uniformly negligible. Indeed, by
Lemma~\ref{lem:product},
\[
  \max_{k\leq n}
  \left|\frac{A_n}{\sqrt n}\Delta M_k\right|
  \leq
  C\max_{k\leq n}\frac{n^a}{\sqrt n\,k^a}
  \leq C n^{a-1/2}
  \longrightarrow0,
\]
where the second inequality uses \(a\geq0\), so \(k^{-a}\leq1\), and the
convergence to zero uses the strict diffusive assumption \(a<1/2\). This is
precisely why the functional theorem is stated only in the range
\(0\leq a<1/2\). The martingale functional central limit theorem
\citep[Chapter 7]{ethier1986markov} therefore yields
\[
  \mathcal M_n
  \Rightarrow
  B\left(\frac{t^{1-2a}}{1-2a}\right)
  \quad\text{in }D[0,1].
\]
The limit is continuous.

It remains to transfer the result from the martingale normalisation to the
original walk. For \(t>0\),
\[
  \frac{S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{\sqrt n}
  =
  \frac{A_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{A_n}\,
  \frac{A_nM_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{\sqrt n}.
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:product} implies
\[
  \frac{A_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{A_n}\to t^a
\]
uniformly on every interval \([\eta,1]\), \(\eta>0\). Near the origin, Doob's
inequality applied to \(M_k\), together with Lemma~\ref{lem:second} and the
bound \(A_k\asymp k^a\), gives
\[
  \E\left[\sup_{0\leq t\leq\eta}
  \left(\frac{S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{\sqrt n}\right)^2\right]
  \leq C\eta.
\]
Thus the contribution of \(t\in[0,\eta]\) can be made uniformly small by first
choosing \(\eta\) small and then letting \(n\to\infty\). The continuous mapping
theorem gives
\[
  Y_n(t)\Rightarrow
  t^a B\left(\frac{t^{1-2a}}{1-2a}\right),
\]
which is the asserted limit. The covariance formula follows immediately from
the Brownian covariance identity
\(\E[B(u)B(v)]=u\wedge v\).
Since the largest jump of \(Y_n\) is at most \(1/\sqrt n\), linear
interpolation changes the path by at most \(1/\sqrt n\) in uniform norm. The
interpolated process therefore has the same weak limit in \(C[0,1]\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Critical learned-memory central limit theorem]\label{thm:critical}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:critical},
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}
  \mathcal N(0,1).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows the same martingale normalisation but the bracket is now
logarithmic. Define \(A_n\) as in Theorem~\ref{thm:martingale}. The proof of
Lemma~\ref{lem:product}, with \(a=1/2\), gives deterministic constants
\(0<c<C<\infty\) such that
\[
  c n^{1/2}\leq A_n\leq C n^{1/2}.
\]
Moreover \(A_n=L n^{1/2}(1+o(1))\) for some \(L\in(0,\infty)\).

We first record the critical second-moment bound. With
\(V_n=\E[S_n^2]\), the computation in Lemma~\ref{lem:second} gives
\[
  V_{n+1}\leq\left(1+\frac1n\right)V_n+1.
\]
Dividing by \(n+1\) yields
\[
  \frac{V_{n+1}}{n+1}\leq \frac{V_n}{n}+\frac{1}{n+1},
\]
and hence \(V_n\leq C n\log n\).

The predictable quadratic variation satisfies
\[
  \langle M\rangle_n
  =
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}
  \frac{1-a_k^2S_k^2/k^2}{A_{k+1}^2}.
\]
Since \(A_k\sim L k^{1/2}\),
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n\log n}\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac1{A_{k+1}^2}
  \longrightarrow 1.
\]
The correction term is negligible because
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \E\left[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n\log n}
  \sum_{k=1}^{n-1}\frac{a_k^2S_k^2/k^2}{A_{k+1}^2}
  \right]
  &\leq
  \frac{C}{\log n}
  \sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\E[S_k^2]}{k^3}  \\
  &\leq
  \frac{C}{\log n}
  \sum_{k=2}^{n}\frac{\log k}{k^2}
  \longrightarrow0.
\end{aligned}
\]
Therefore
\[
  \frac{A_n^2}{n\log n}\langle M\rangle_n
  \xrightarrow{\Pbb}1.
\]
The martingale Lindeberg condition follows from
\(|\Delta M_{k+1}|\leq2/A_{k+1}\) and
\(\langle M\rangle_n\asymp\log n\), since
\[
  \max_{k\leq n}
  \frac{|\Delta M_k|}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \leq \frac{C}{\sqrt{\log n}}
  \longrightarrow0.
\]
The martingale central limit theorem and Slutsky's theorem now give
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}
  =
  \frac{M_n}{\langle M\rangle_n^{1/2}}
  \left(\frac{A_n^2\langle M\rangle_n}{n\log n}\right)^{1/2}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}\mathcal N(0,1).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Critical functional limit]\label{thm:critical-fclt}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:critical}, the cĂ dlĂ g process
\[
  Z_n(t)=\frac{S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}}{\sqrt{n\log n}},
  \qquad 0\leq t\leq1,
\]
converges in finite-dimensional distributions to the rank-one Gaussian process
\[
  Z(t)=\sqrt t\,N,
  \qquad 0\leq t\leq1,
\]
where \(N\sim\mathcal N(0,1)\). Its covariance is
\[
  \E[Z(s)Z(t)]=\sqrt{st}.
\]
Moreover, the linearly interpolated version of \(Z_n\) is tight in \(C[0,1]\),
and every subsequential limit has the above finite-dimensional distributions.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(0<t_1<\cdots<t_m\leq1\) and real numbers
\(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_m\). By Cramer--Wold, it is enough to identify the
limit of
\[
  \sum_{j=1}^{m}\lambda_j
  \frac{S_{\lfloor nt_j\rfloor}}{\sqrt{n\log n}}.
\]
Using \(S_k=A_kM_k\) and \(A_{\lfloor nt_j\rfloor}/A_n\to\sqrt{t_j}\), this
linear combination equals
\[
  \left(\sum_{j=1}^{m}\lambda_j\sqrt{t_j}\right)
  \frac{A_nM_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}+R_n,
\]
where \(R_n\to0\) in probability. Indeed, the additional martingale increment
from \(\lfloor nt_j\rfloor\) to \(n\) contributes only
\[
  \frac{A_{\lfloor nt_j\rfloor}}{\sqrt{n\log n}}
  (M_n-M_{\lfloor nt_j\rfloor}),
\]
whose variance is \(O(1/\log n)\), because the corresponding bracket increment
is \(O(1)\). The critical CLT in Theorem~\ref{thm:critical} gives
\[
  \frac{A_nM_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}
  =
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt{n\log n}}
  \Rightarrow N.
\]
Thus the finite-dimensional distributions converge to those of
\(\sqrt t\,N\).

For tightness of the linearly interpolated processes, let \(0\leq s<t\leq1\).
The same martingale decomposition and the critical second-moment estimate in
Theorem~\ref{thm:critical} give
\[
  \E\left[
  \left(
  \frac{S_{\lfloor nt\rfloor}-S_{\lfloor ns\rfloor}}
       {\sqrt{n\log n}}
  \right)^2
  \right]
  \leq C|\sqrt t-\sqrt s|^2+\frac{C}{\log n}.
\]
Kolmogorov--Chentsov's criterion applied away from the origin, together with
the bound
\(\E[S_{\lfloor n\eta\rfloor}^2]/(n\log n)\leq C\eta\), yields tightness.
The jump size is at most \(1/\sqrt{n\log n}\), so interpolation does not change
the weak limit.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quenched universality of stable learned memory]\label{cor:quenched}
Let \(\mathcal A(a,\varepsilon,\bar a,D)\) be the class of predictable memory
coefficient paths \((a_n)\) satisfying Assumption~\ref{ass:stable} with the same
constants \(a,\varepsilon,\bar a,D\). For every realised learning path in
\(\mathcal A(a,\varepsilon,\bar a,D)\), the terminal diffusive limit
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt n}
  \Rightarrow
  \mathcal N\left(0,\frac{1}{1-2a}\right)
\]
holds with the same variance. If additionally \(0\leq a<1/2\), the functional
limit in Theorem~\ref{thm:fclt} also holds with the same Gaussian process.
Consequently, within this stable class, the first-order diffusive asymptotics
are universal: they depend on the learned limit \(a\), but not on the particular
online algorithm or on the transient trajectory by which \(a_n\) approaches
\(a\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The proofs of Lemmas~\ref{lem:product}--\ref{lem:lindeberg},
Theorem~\ref{thm:clt}, and Theorem~\ref{thm:fclt} use the learning path only
through the deterministic bounds and summability constants appearing in
Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}. Once a realised path belongs to
\(\mathcal A(a,\varepsilon,\bar a,D)\), the same estimates apply pathwise to
that environment. Thus the limiting variance and covariance kernel are fixed
by \(a\) alone.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Stability of convergent learning policies]\label{cor:ai}
Let \(\theta_n\in\R^d\) be an \(\F_n\)-predictable learning state and let
\(\ell:\R^d\to(\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon)\) be Lipschitz. Put
\[
  p_n=\ell(\theta_n),\qquad a_n=2p_n-1.
\]
If \(\theta_n\to\theta_\star\) almost surely and there is
\(D_\theta<\infty\) such that
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\|\theta_n-\theta_\star\|}{n}\leq D_\theta
  \qquad\text{almost surely},
\]
with \(a=2\ell(\theta_\star)-1<1/2\), and if there exists
\(\bar a<1/2\) such that \(2\ell(\theta_n)-1\leq\bar a\) for all \(n\), then
the LC-ERW satisfies the central limit theorem in Theorem~\ref{thm:clt}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Lipschitz continuity,
\[
  |a_n-a|\leq 2\,\operatorname{Lip}(\ell)\|\theta_n-\theta_\star\|.
\]
The displayed summability condition implies Assumption~\ref{ass:stable}, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:clt} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Projected online empirical-risk learner]\label{prop:damped}
Let \(I\subset\R\) be a compact interval, let \(\Pi_I\) denote Euclidean
projection onto \(I\), and let \(\ell:I\to(\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon)\) be
Lipschitz. Fix \(\theta_\star\in I\), \(c,\beta\in\R\), \(\rho>0\) and
\(\delta>0\). At time \(n\), define the online regularised loss
\[
  \mathcal L_n(\theta)
  =
  \frac12\left\{\theta-\theta_\star-c(n+1)^{-\rho}\right\}^2
  +\beta\theta\frac{S_n}{n^{1+\delta}},
  \qquad \theta\in I,
\]
and choose the learning state
\[
  \theta_n
  =
  \arg\min_{\theta\in I}\mathcal L_n(\theta)
  =
  \Pi_I\left(
  \theta_\star+c(n+1)^{-\rho}
  -\beta\frac{S_n}{n^{1+\delta}}
  \right),
  \qquad n\geq1,
\]
with \(p_n=\ell(\theta_n)\). Suppose that
\[
  a=2\ell(\theta_\star)-1<1/2
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \sup_{\theta\in I}\{2\ell(\theta)-1\}\leq\bar a<1/2.
\]
Then the resulting LC-ERW satisfies
\[
  \frac{S_n}{\sqrt n}
  \xrightarrow{\mathcal D}
  \mathcal N\left(0,\frac{1}{1-2a}\right).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The loss \(\mathcal L_n\) is \(\F_n\)-measurable and strongly convex on the
compact decision set \(I\), so \(\theta_n\) is a predictable online decision.
Projection is non-expansive and \(\Pi_I(\theta_\star)=\theta_\star\). Since
\(|S_n|\leq n\),
\[
  |\theta_n-\theta_\star|
  \leq
  \left|c(n+1)^{-\rho}-\beta\frac{S_n}{n^{1+\delta}}\right|
  \leq |c|(n+1)^{-\rho}+|\beta|n^{-\delta}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{|\theta_n-\theta_\star|}{n}
  \leq
  |c|\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n(n+1)^\rho}
  +|\beta|\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{1+\delta}}
  <\infty.
\]
The result follows from Corollary~\ref{cor:ai}. This proposition gives a
concrete online learning rule: the controller solves a one-step regularised
empirical-risk problem, reacts to the observed empirical drift, and is projected
onto a policy class that is known in advance to remain diffusive.
\end{proof}

\section{Examples of Learning Rules}

\paragraph{Deterministic annealing.}
Let \(p_n=p_\star+c(n+1)^{-\rho}\), truncated to
\((\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon)\), where \(p_\star<3/4\) and \(\rho>0\).
Then \(a_n\to a=2p_\star-1<1/2\) and the summability condition holds.

\paragraph{Online logistic policy.}
Let \(p_n=\ell(\theta_n)\), where
\(\ell(x)=\varepsilon+(1-2\varepsilon)/(1+\exp(-x))\), and suppose
\(\theta_n\) is an online estimator converging to \(\theta_\star\) at a
polynomial rate. The corollary gives the limiting law whenever
\(\ell(\theta_\star)<3/4\).

\paragraph{Projected empirical-risk learning.}
The rule in Proposition~\ref{prop:damped} is deliberately simple but genuinely
algorithmic: at each time it solves a projected, strongly convex online learning
problem. The empirical-drift term uses \(S_n/n\), while the additional
\(n^{-\delta}\) damping makes the stability condition verifiable without an
extra stochastic-approximation theorem.

\paragraph{Stochastic approximation policies.}
More elaborate controllers can be analysed by first proving convergence of
\(\theta_n\) through standard stochastic approximation arguments
\citep{kushner2003stochastic}, and then applying Corollary~\ref{cor:ai}. This
two-step route is useful when the policy parameter is updated by a projected
Robbins--Monro recursion or by a bounded online estimator.

\section{Discussion}

The LC-ERW shows that an adaptive memory policy can be incorporated into an ERW
without destroying its martingale backbone. The functional theorem is stronger
than a terminal-time CLT: it identifies the whole diffusive scaling limit and
therefore the covariance structure induced by learned long memory. The critical
theorems show that the learned process also preserves the ERW phase boundary:
when \(a_n\to1/2\), the correct normalisation is \(\sqrt{n\log n}\), and the
critical path limit collapses to the rank-one Gaussian process \(\sqrt t\,N\).
In the stable diffusive regime, predictable learning is asymptotically
equivalent to freezing the memory parameter at its learned limit, but only after
the correct non-Markovian time change has been taken into account. This is
useful in two directions. For probability theory, it extends ERW analysis from
fixed or externally prescribed parameters to predictable data-driven policies.
For online learning, it gives a rare case in which a complete-memory adaptive
exploration mechanism has explicit diffusive and critical limit laws.

The quenched universality corollary is the key conceptual point. It says that a
large class of learning algorithms collapses, at diffusive scale, to a single
universality class indexed by \(a\). The transient learning trajectory may be
deterministic, estimator-driven or feedback-driven, but once it satisfies the
stable summability condition it leaves no first-order trace in the Gaussian
limit. This separates the present result from models in which the memory set,
replacement matrix or reinforcement weights are themselves the asymptotic state
variables.

Several limitations remain. The functional theorem is stated for
\(0\leq a<1/2\); extending it to negative learned memory coefficients should be
possible but requires a sharper treatment of the origin. Superdiffusive limits
\(a>1/2\), multidimensional ERW, and learning rules whose parameter does not
converge are also outside the present scope. These cases are likely to require
different normalisations and may lead to non-Gaussian limits. Another open
direction is statistical inference: estimating the learned memory trajectory
\(p_n\) from observed LC-ERW paths.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper formulated a learning-controlled elephant random walk and proved
that predictable adaptive memory policies admit an exact martingale
normalisation. Under a stable diffusive learning condition, the process obeys
both a scalar central limit theorem and a functional invariance principle with
an explicit Gaussian limit. At the learned critical boundary, the same
martingale method gives a central limit theorem with the classical
\(\sqrt{n\log n}\) normalisation and a path-level rank-one Gaussian limit. The
result gives a rigorous and reproducible starting point for connecting elephant
random walks with online learning-controlled stochastic exploration.
